Unit Economics, Business Model, and Pricing Strategy

Economics (WaveShift / Sentinel-ASV)

WaveShift is built to win on unit economics + repeatable deployments, not on flashy one-off hardware.

1) Business Model: "Paid pilots → annual service contracts"

Core thesis: ports don't want to buy a boat — they want persistent coverage with measurable outcomes and predictable cost.

What we sell (recurring)

Sentinel-as-a-Service (hardware-enabled recurring revenue):

  • Annual per-site contract (coverage + response workflow + reporting)
  • Optional add-on vessels as coverage expands (per-vessel recurring)
  • Software + operator console + monitoring ops + maintenance bundled into one service

Why this is not one-off hardware sales

Hardware is the delivery vehicle, but the value is:

  • Coverage hours (persistent presence without staffing a crew)
  • Detection → verification → response workflow (human-in-the-loop)
  • Evidence & audit trail (incident clips, tracks, heatmaps, compliance logs)
  • Reliability over time (maintenance, updates, spares, uptime SLAs)

This structure is intentionally closer to security services + SaaS than "marine robotics as a product."

2) First Product Configuration: Sentinel-ASV (Monitoring)

A practical first configuration: port monitoring + patrol + verification, designed to plug into existing security operations.

Included (baseline monitoring configuration)

  • Uncrewed surface vessel platform (hull + propulsion + power management)
  • Comms + remote ops stack (secure link; LTE/5G where available; satellite backup where required)
  • Core sensing for MDA-lite:
    • Marine radar (surface track)
    • EO/IR for visual verification (day/night)
    • AIS receiver/transponder options (policy-dependent)
  • Autonomy & safety features:
    • Geofencing & patrol routes
    • Collision-avoidance behaviors
    • Failsafes (RTB / loiter / safe-stop)
  • Operator console (web UI + alerting + replay)
  • Deployment package:
    • Commissioning, playbooks, training
    • Maintenance schedule + remote diagnostics

Not included (by design, early phase)

  • Armed payloads or weapons integration
  • High-end mission sets (mines, ASW, etc.)
  • Full "national defense" scope claims

WaveShift's early product is intentionally commercial + compliance-friendly: monitor, verify, document, coordinate response.

3) Unit Economics (v1): BOM range, annual ops + software, and crewed-patrol comparison

A) Hardware BOM (per vessel)

A realistic build range depends mainly on sensor suite + comms + redundancy:

Estimated BOM range: ~$75k – $250k+

  • Radar: common marine radars are in the low-thousands to ~10k+ range depending on capability (example: Garmin open-array radar systems listed around $8,399.99).
  • EO/IR payload: ranges widely — from ~$10k "entry stabilized EO/IR" class units to far higher-end systems.
  • Connectivity: Starlink Maritime shows hardware ~$1,999 and service starting around $250/mo (plan-dependent).
  • AIS: typical transponder pricing spans roughly $200–$2,000+ depending on class/features.

(WaveShift pricing doesn't require bleeding-edge sensors on day 1 — it requires reliable detection → verification at port ranges.)

B) Annual ops + software (per vessel)

Estimated annual run-rate (per deployed vessel): ~$40k – $150k

Key drivers:

  • Connectivity (cellular + satellite backup where needed)
  • Maintenance & spares (marine environment realities)
  • Insurance/compliance, docking/charging logistics
  • Software + monitoring + updates + operator training

C) Comparison to crewed patrol cost (why this pencils immediately)

Crewing is expensive because it stacks labor + fuel + scheduling + utilization inefficiency.

A useful, conservative anchor: the U.S. Coast Guard's published reimbursable standard rate table includes a "Transportable Port Security Boat" with a total hourly rate shown at $3,761/hr (inside government) and $4,774/hr (outside government).

WaveShift doesn't need to "beat" that hourly number on day one to win. It needs to:

  • provide more coverage hours
  • with less operator burden
  • at a predictable annual contract

4) Pricing Logic: per-site contracts + per-vessel range

Per-site contract (base)

What the port buys: guaranteed coverage capacity + reporting + integration into their workflow.

A clean structure buyers understand:

  • Base site subscription: covers platform availability + software + reporting + ops support
  • Per-vessel add-ons: expand patrol area, add redundancy, extend hours, cover multiple terminals

Per-vessel pricing range (simple and defensible)

Typical service pricing target: $250k – $600k per vessel-year (service contract)

(Varies by coverage hours, sensor suite, response workflow, and SLA.)

Why customers can justify it

They're not comparing to "buying a boat." They're comparing to:

  • staffing / contracting crews (overtime, turnover, compliance)
  • fuel + maintenance + downtime
  • limited coverage hours
  • lack of auditability and measurable outcomes

If a small crewed security boat can effectively cost thousands per hour on a fully-loaded basis in official reimbursable tables, ports can justify a contract that delivers persistent coverage with fewer human hours.

5) SOM

WaveShift's early SOM is intentionally tight and operationally realistic:

Initial SOM definition

  • 20–50 ports
  • 1–5 vessels per port
  • $5M–$15M early revenue

Revenue Projections

If early deployments average ~2 vessels per port and ~$250k–$400k per vessel-year, then:

  • 20 ports × 2 vessels × $250k = $10M ARR
  • 50 ports × 1 vessel × $250k = $12.5M ARR
  • 20 ports × 3 vessels × $250k = $15M ARR

This is why WaveShift starts with monitoring/patrol: it's a repeatable deployment pattern across many ports.

6) Buyer Success Metrics

WaveShift should sell with a dashboard that ties directly to outcomes:

Cost & coverage

  • Cost per coverage hour (all-in)
  • Verified coverage hours per week (by zone/terminal)
  • Coverage density (hours per square mile / per berth)

Operator workload

  • Alerts per hour (and false-positive rate)
  • Time-to-verify (alert → EO/IR confirmation)
  • Human minutes per patrol hour (goal: compress workload)

Operational effectiveness

  • Time-to-detect and time-to-verify
  • Incident documentation completeness (clip + track + report)
  • Deterrence indicators (repeat incidents trend down, faster response coordination)
